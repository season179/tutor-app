Center column · full tutoring flow

Coach Echo, where the lesson actually happens

The center column is the stage for one tutoring session, end to end. It is built to make the converged workflow visible to a 7–12-year-old and a watching parent — while making it structurally hard to leak the answer. Everything below uses the app's real sage-green design system; these are proposed states, not shipped code.

How to read the verifier feedback

Correct — only on checkable claims, always paired with the reason (“because…”).
◐Partial — warm amber, names the missing piece, never a failure.
Try again — muted, never red, with an instant warm redirect.
“good thinking”Open reasoning — “what do you notice?” gets engagement warmth and no correctness mark at all.

Frame the task — the gate (the screen that earns its keep)

Solving stays out of reach until the child shows they understand. The later stations sit ghosted (“comes next”, no punitive lock); the empty target chip is the north star. 03

Step loop — your turn (the heart, repeats per step)

The gate just unlocked: “Understand” shows a tick, “Work it out” lit up, and the target chip is now framed — the north star for the rest of the problem. A checkable step earned a ✓ with a reason. There's exactly one ask, a quiet scaffold aid, the mic in its listening state, and help is always one tap away (“Give me a hint”, “Park this”) — but there is no countdown.

04

Step loop — a wrong answer, handled kindly

A checkable miss gets “try again” — never a red stamp — and an immediate, specific redirect. The wait timer is invisible: after the age-banded pause the mic enters a calm breathing “take your time” state and a hint quietly surfaces. Support has climbed, so the scaffold aid is more concrete. “Park this” is always there so a step can never trap the child.

05

Answer check — back to the framed goal

The loop closes where it opened. The pin re-expands, the target chip goes complete, and the check is a sanity test (does 6 × 4 = 24?) plus the required output language: the worksheet is Malay, so the final sentence must be Malay even though the child reasoned in English. The win is tied to the child's reasoning, not Echo's. (Memory write — “what helped you figure it out?” — happens right after, then the problem is done; no forced second problem.)

06

Transfer check — on your own

Solving with help is evidence, not proof. Near the close, one fresh item with reduced help: the hint and park buttons are gone, and the room takes an “on your own” tone (a fresh item still opens with a quick “what's it asking?” micro-orient — not depicted here). Passing this is what promotes mastery. It's fully skippable if the child is tired — mastery just stays “developing”, no shame.

07

Wrap-up — the child names what they learned

The child summarizes, Echo assigns one small practice task and previews next time. The goal chip is marked achieved. A mastery change is logged for the parent (subtle), never announced to the child as a level-up. This summary becomes the input to the next session's open.

One open call for the next iteration. The gate (frame 02) is the one place the two designs split. A literal padlock was rejected — it can feel punitive to an anxious child, or a challenge to “break” for a rushing one. The shipped reading uses soft “comes next” ghosting plus the empty target chip, with the rewarding “lights up” moment when the gate passes (frame 03). The recommended refinement: make the empty target chip the visible lock — enlarge “We need to find ___” as the hero of frame 02 so filling it clearly is the unlock, since ghosting alone may read as “progress” rather than “locked”. Everything else converged.
